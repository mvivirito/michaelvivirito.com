## Save #1: locked out mid-move

I was moving apartments, hauling boxes into the new place, when I managed to lock myself out of the exact room I was moving into. Keys on the wrong side of the door, arms full of stuff. The saving grace: the pick set was packed in one of the boxes I'd already carried inside. I dug it out, worked the lock, and was back in within a few minutes. The thing I'd bought as a novelty paid for itself the first time it actually mattered.

## Save #2: keys and phone locked in with the bike

These days I ride a Specialized Sirrus 1.0, and I keep it in a garage secured with a Master padlock. One afternoon I managed to lock my keys *and* my phone in that garage, zipped into a little backpack, with the bike. No phone, no keys, just me and a padlock.

The lucky part: my wife was home, so I could at least get back into the house. I grabbed the kit, pulled up a quick YouTube video for the technique on that style of Master lock, and had it open in about five minutes. red team baby.

## Why a cheap lock pick set earns its spot

A few reasons one belongs in the junk drawer:

- **It's cheap insurance:** a locksmith is real money and a real wait. A pick set is a few dollars and sits in a drawer until the day it saves you both.
- **Everyday locks are softer than they look:** a standard Master padlock has a handful of pins and opens with a tension wrench and a hook, which is exactly why you shouldn't trust one to guard anything that really matters.
- **Knowing how things fail is a skill:** that's the red-team mindset. Understanding how a lock is beaten tells you what's genuinely secure versus what just looks secure. The same instinct that makes you a better defender of a network makes you side-eye the cheap padlock on your own garage.

## One important note

Pick locks you own, or ones you have explicit permission to pick. That's the whole rule. Practicing on your own padlocks (or a see-through practice lock) is a fun and legal hobby in most places, but the laws vary by where you live, so know yours. The goal is understanding your own security, not bypassing anyone else's.
